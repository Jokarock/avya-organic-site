Our Story

Rooted in nature,
built with purpose

AVYA began with a simple frustration — most self-care products are made from plastic, loaded with synthetics, and designed to be thrown away. We believed there was a better way.

Our bath collection sources directly from craftspeople who have worked with bamboo, sisal, and beechwood for generations. Every brush is made to be used with intention, loved through ritual, and eventually returned to the earth. No microplastics. No shortcuts.

Our bag collection is rooted in India’s extraordinary handcraft heritage — block-printed cotton, hand-knotted macrame, and natural hemp, made by skilled artisans using techniques passed down across generations. Each bag carries the fingerprint of the hands that made it.

Two very different categories. One philosophy: choose materials honestly, make things beautifully, and let nothing go to waste.

What We Stand For

Our Commitments

🌿
100% Natural
No synthetics, no shortcuts. Every material selected for its natural origin and biodegradable end-of-life.
🌿
Bamboo First
Fast-growing, carbon-sequestering, endlessly renewable. Bamboo is the backbone of our bath collection.
🤝
Artisan Heritage
Every bag traces its origin to skilled craftspeople using techniques passed down across generations in India.
♻
Zero Waste Intent
Packaging and materials chosen to leave the lightest possible trace on the earth we all share.
Sourced · Southeast Asia
Moso Bamboo
The fastest-growing plant on earth. Our bamboo handles are harvested from sustainably managed forests, laser-etched with our branding, and naturally antibacterial. Each handle is unique — no two have the same grain.
Sourced · Mexico & Africa
Natural Sisal
Harvested from the agave plant, sisal is one of the world’s strongest natural fibres. Our sisal bristles provide firm, effective exfoliation that improves circulation and removes dead skin — without a single gram of plastic.
Crafted · Rajasthan, India
Cotton & Hemp
Our bags are made from unbleached cotton canvas and raw hemp fibre sourced from small-scale Indian farms. Block-printing is done by hand using vegetable dyes — each print slightly different, each bag genuinely one of a kind.
